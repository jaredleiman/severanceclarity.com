IS SEVERANCE PAY TAXABLE?

April 2026·7 min read·SeveranceClarity

Yes — severance pay is taxable income. But how it's taxed, what gets withheld, and what you might owe at tax time depends on factors most employees don't think about until after they've already spent the money.

THE BASICS

Severance pay is treated as ordinary income for federal tax purposes. It is subject to federal income tax withholding, Social Security tax, Medicare tax, and applicable state income taxes. The IRS treats severance the same as wages — it goes on your W-2 for the year in which it was paid.

Federal Income Tax
22–37%
Withheld at your marginal rate or at the 22% supplemental wage rate if paid separately from regular wages
Social Security Tax
6.2%
Applied up to the annual wage base limit ($168,600 in 2026). May not apply if you've already hit the cap.
Medicare Tax
1.45%
No wage cap. High earners (above $200K single) pay an additional 0.9% surtax on the excess.

HOW WITHHOLDING WORKS

How your severance is paid affects how taxes are withheld:

Lump Sum Payment

If your severance is paid as a lump sum separately from your final paycheck, the IRS allows employers to withhold at the supplemental wage rate of 22% (or 37% if the total supplemental wages paid to you in the year exceed $1 million). This is a flat rate — it may be higher or lower than your actual marginal rate, which means you may owe additional tax or receive a refund when you file.

Salary Continuation

If your severance is paid as salary continuation — regular payroll payments over a defined period — it's typically withheld using your standard withholding method (W-4 elections). This more closely tracks your actual tax liability but results in smaller, periodic payments rather than a lump sum.

What This Means for Your Tax Return

Regardless of how withholding happens, your severance will appear on your W-2 as wages. At tax filing time, your actual tax liability is calculated on your total annual income — salary you earned earlier in the year, severance, any freelance income, investment income, etc. If withholding was insufficient, you'll owe the difference. If excess was withheld, you'll receive a refund.

THE UNEMPLOYMENT INTERACTION

Receiving severance pay can affect your unemployment benefit eligibility and amount in some states. Some states treat severance as wages paid for a future period, which can delay the start of unemployment benefits. Others don't count severance against unemployment eligibility at all. Check your state's specific rules — and when you file for unemployment, disclose your severance accurately.

NEGOTIATING WITH TAXES IN MIND

Timing Can Matter

If you're laid off late in the year and have already earned significant income, receiving a large lump-sum severance in the same tax year pushes more income into higher brackets. Requesting to receive part of your severance in the following calendar year — as salary continuation rather than lump sum — can sometimes reduce your total tax liability. This requires your employer's agreement and careful implementation.

Retirement Contributions

In some cases, severance can be contributed to a 401(k) or other qualified retirement plan, which would reduce your taxable income for the year. Whether this is allowed depends on your plan document and your employer's payroll setup. Ask your HR department before assuming this is an option.

Tax Planning Note

Understanding the tax implications of your severance is important for cash flow planning — but tax optimization strategies should be discussed with a qualified CPA or tax advisor, not an employment attorney or document analysis service.
