SEVERANCE AGREEMENT RED FLAGS

April 2026·9 min read·SeveranceClarity

Most severance agreements are standard. But some contain clauses that are unusually one-sided, overly broad, or carry consequences most employees don't anticipate. Here are 7 red flags to look for before you sign.

WHY THIS MATTERS

When you sign a severance agreement, you're entering a legally binding contract. The fact that your employer prepared it doesn't mean it's fair, standard, or balanced. Some provisions that appear in severance agreements can significantly restrict your future employment, expose you to financial penalties, or waive rights that you'd want to preserve.

Understanding what you're signing — before you sign — is the entire point. Here are the 7 clauses most worth scrutinizing.

THE 7 RED FLAGS

01
An Overbroad Non-Compete
A non-compete that restricts you from working in your entire industry, across a wide geographic area, for an extended period (more than 12 months) is a red flag — especially if you're an individual contributor, not an executive with access to trade secrets. Non-competes vary significantly in enforceability by state, but an overbroad one can still create practical employment obstacles even if legally unenforceable.
02
A One-Sided Non-Disparagement Clause
Non-disparagement is standard in both directions — you don't disparage them, they don't disparage you. A red flag is when the clause binds you but not the company, or when it's so broad that it would prevent you from honestly describing your job duties to future employers or responding to reference check questions. Look for whether the company has a reciprocal obligation.
03
An Unusually Broad Cooperation Clause
Cooperation clauses require you to assist your former employer with litigation, investigations, or audits after you leave. A reasonable clause limits this to matters you were personally involved in during your employment. A red flag clause imposes open-ended cooperation obligations with no time limit, no compensation for your time, and penalties for non-cooperation.
04
A Clawback With Vague Triggers
Clawback provisions allow your employer to demand repayment of severance under certain conditions — typically if you violate the agreement. A red flag is when the triggers are vague ("any conduct detrimental to the company") rather than specific. Vague clawback triggers give your employer broad discretion to demand repayment for conduct that may be entirely lawful.
05
Waiver of Future Claims, Not Just Past
Standard severance agreements release claims arising from your employment — past conduct. A red flag is language that purports to release claims arising after the agreement is signed, or that is so broad it could be read to cover future conduct. Courts often void these provisions, but the language itself signals an aggressive posture from the employer.
06
Missing ADEA Compliance Language (If You're 40+)
If you're 40 or older, federal law (the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act) requires specific language in your agreement — including an explicit reference to your right to consult an attorney, the 21-day review period, and the 7-day revocation right after signing. Missing any of these elements means the waiver of ADEA claims may be legally invalid — but you should understand this before, not after, signing.
07
An Artificial Deadline Shorter Than 21 Days (If You're 40+)
If you're 40 or older, the ADEA requires a minimum 21-day review period. Some employers pressure employees to sign faster — "we need this back by end of week" — even when they're legally required to give you 21 days. This pressure tactic is worth recognizing. You do not have to comply with an artificially shortened deadline that violates your statutory rights.
What To Do If You Spot a Red Flag

Finding a concerning clause doesn't mean you should automatically reject the agreement. It means you need to understand exactly what it says, what it means for your specific situation, and whether it's negotiable. Most red flag clauses can be modified — if you ask before you sign. Once you sign, they're binding.
