Severance Guide

STOCK OPTIONS & RSUS WHEN LAID OFF

April 2026·8 min read·SeveranceClarity

Equity treatment in a severance agreement can be worth more than the cash payment — or nothing, depending on what you had, what was vested, and what your agreement says. This is one of the most financially significant and least understood parts of any severance deal.

THE FUNDAMENTAL RULE

Vested equity is yours. Whatever was vested on your last day of employment — vested stock options you can exercise, RSUs that have already settled — the company cannot take that back through a severance agreement. What a severance agreement can affect is your unvested equity, the timeline for exercising options, and what happens to equity in specific termination scenarios.

BY EQUITY TYPE

RSUs
Default: Unvested RSUs forfeit
Restricted Stock Units that haven't vested by your termination date are typically forfeited. The question is whether your severance accelerates any vesting — some companies offer partial acceleration as part of a negotiated departure.
ISOs
Default: 90-day exercise window
Incentive Stock Options typically must be exercised within 90 days of termination or they expire (and may convert to NSOs). Your severance may extend this window — a critical negotiation point if your options are valuable but you can't afford to exercise immediately.
NSOs
Default: 90-day window (varies)
Non-Qualified Stock Options have an exercise window set by the plan document. Unlike ISOs, there's no statutory 90-day rule — the plan controls. Many plans do default to 90 days post-termination.
ESPP
Shares held; enrollment ends
Employee Stock Purchase Plan shares already purchased are yours. Your enrollment in the plan terminates when employment ends, and any accumulated payroll deductions for the current purchase period may be refunded rather than used to purchase shares.

WHAT YOUR SEVERANCE AGREEMENT MAY SAY ABOUT EQUITY

Your severance agreement may address equity in several ways:

Acceleration of Unvested Equity

Some severance packages include partial or full acceleration of unvested RSUs or options. Single-trigger acceleration (vesting upon termination) is most common when the layoff is due to a company event like a reduction in force. This is more negotiable than most employees realize — particularly if you're being let go close to a vesting cliff.

Extended Exercise Window

For employees with valuable stock options, an extended exercise window (from 90 days to 1–3 years) can be enormously valuable — especially if the company is private and there's no immediate liquidity event. This is a point worth raising in severance negotiations for any employee with significant option holdings.

The Forfeiture Provision

Some severance agreements include provisions that forfeit already-vested equity if you violate the agreement — particularly the non-disparagement or non-compete clauses. This is a red flag worth examining carefully. Some of these provisions are unenforceable depending on state law, but they create practical risk.

The Cliff Problem

If you're laid off just before a vesting cliff — a date when a large block of equity vests — the financial stakes of your severance negotiation are significantly higher. An extra 30 days of employment or an acceleration provision could mean tens of thousands of dollars in equity that either vests or doesn't. This is worth raising explicitly with an employment attorney before you sign.

WHAT TO LOOK FOR IN YOUR AGREEMENT

  • Does the agreement address equity at all? If not, you're governed by the original grant documents — find them
  • What does it say about unvested equity? Does anything accelerate, or does everything unvested simply forfeit?
  • What is the exercise window for options? Is it the plan default or a negotiated extension?
  • Are there any forfeiture provisions on vested equity? Under what conditions could you lose vested shares or options?
  • What happens to equity in a future change of control? Some agreements address whether you'd participate in a future acquisition if it happens within a certain period after your departure

WHEN TO GET PROFESSIONAL HELP

If you have significant equity holdings, this is the area where an employment attorney's time is most clearly justified. The tax implications of equity — particularly the interaction between ISO exercise timing and the alternative minimum tax — also warrant a conversation with a tax professional. The equity section of a severance agreement can easily be worth more than the legal fees to review it properly.
