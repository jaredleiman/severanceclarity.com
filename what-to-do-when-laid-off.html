Layoff Guide

WHAT TO DO WHEN YOU GET LAID OFF

April 2026·9 min read·SeveranceClarity

Getting laid off is disorienting. Most people's instinct is to either sign whatever they're given to make the process end, or to freeze entirely. Neither serves you well. The first 72 hours matter enormously — here's exactly what to do.

THE FIRST HOUR

When you're told you're being laid off, your employer controls the environment. They've prepared for this conversation. You haven't. The most important thing you can do in the first few minutes is resist pressure to make immediate decisions.

⚠ Do Not Sign Anything Today

You will almost certainly be handed a severance agreement during or shortly after the termination conversation. Do not sign it on the spot. You have time — typically 21 days under federal law if you're over 40, and most employers allow reasonable review time for all employees regardless of age. Politely acknowledge receipt and tell them you need to review it.

THE FIRST 24 HOURS

  • 01
    Get everything in writing
    Ask for written confirmation of your termination date, your severance offer, any continuation of benefits, and any outstanding compensation (bonus, commissions, unused PTO). If your employer communicated anything verbally, follow up with an email summarizing what you understood to be offered.
  • 02
    Document the circumstances
    Write down exactly what was said during the termination conversation — who was in the room, what reason was given, and any comments that stood out. Do this while your memory is fresh. This documentation matters if you later believe the termination was discriminatory or retaliatory.
  • 03
    Secure your personal documents
    Before you lose system access (which often happens within hours of a layoff), make sure you have copies of any personal documents — your own performance reviews, offer letter, comp statements, and any communications relevant to your employment that you're entitled to retain. Do not take confidential company information.
  • 04
    File for unemployment
    You can file for unemployment benefits the same day you're laid off. Do not wait. In most states, there's a waiting period before benefits begin — the clock doesn't start until you file. Filing immediately also preserves your eligibility window.

WHAT TO ASK YOUR EMPLOYER

Before you leave, or in a follow-up email, make sure you have answers to these questions:

  • What is my official last day of employment? This affects your benefits continuation start date
  • When does my health insurance coverage end? And will COBRA paperwork be sent?
  • What happens to my unvested equity? Stock options, RSUs, and similar compensation
  • Will I receive payment for unused PTO? This depends on your state and company policy
  • Is the severance offer negotiable? (It often is — more on this below)
  • Are there any conditions that would void my severance? Read the agreement carefully for clawback provisions

WHAT NOT TO DO

Don't post on social media

Your severance agreement almost certainly contains a non-disparagement clause. Venting about your employer on LinkedIn or Twitter before you understand your agreement's terms could constitute a violation — even if your post is factually accurate. Wait until you've read and understood your agreement.

Don't assume the offer is final

Most severance offers are negotiable. The first offer is rarely the best offer. Common items that get improved through negotiation: total severance amount, extended benefits continuation, outplacement services, and the reference letter terms. You lose nothing by asking.

Don't ignore the deadline

Your severance agreement has a signing deadline. If you're 40 or older, federal law (the ADEA) requires a minimum 21-day review period. If you're under 40, there may be a shorter deadline in your agreement. Know when your deadline is and plan accordingly — getting an extension is possible but not guaranteed.

About Your Severance Agreement

A severance agreement is a legal contract. Signing it permanently waives significant rights — including the right to sue your employer for many claims. Understanding exactly what you're waiving before you sign is the single most important step in the layoff process. That's what SeveranceClarity is built to help with.

THE 72-HOUR CHECKLIST

  1. Get termination details and severance offer in writing
  2. Document the circumstances of your termination
  3. Secure personal documents before losing system access
  4. File for unemployment immediately
  5. Review your health insurance options (COBRA, marketplace, spouse plan)
  6. Locate your original offer letter and employment agreement
  7. Ask clarifying questions about equity, PTO, and benefit continuation
  8. Read your severance agreement — or get it explained before signing
