Severance Guide

IS MY SEVERANCE PACKAGE FAIR?

April 2026·8 min read·SeveranceClarity

Most employees have no baseline for evaluating whether their severance offer is fair. Employers count on this. Here's how to benchmark your offer before you sign — and what's typically negotiable.

THE STANDARD FORMULA

There is no federal law requiring employers to offer severance pay. Most severance packages are discretionary — but they follow recognizable patterns that you can benchmark against.

The most common formula is one to two weeks of base pay per year of service. So an employee with 8 years at a company might expect 8–16 weeks of severance. Senior employees and executives typically receive more — often 3–4 weeks per year of service, sometimes with a minimum floor (e.g., "no less than 12 weeks").

WHAT ELSE GOES INTO THE OFFER

Raw weeks of pay is only one dimension of a severance offer. A complete evaluation should consider:

Benefits Continuation

How long does your employer continue health, dental, and vision coverage after your last day? Some offers extend COBRA subsidy for 1–3 months. Others terminate coverage on your last day and leave you to pay full COBRA premiums immediately (which can run $600–$1,200/month for a family). The difference in value between these scenarios can be thousands of dollars.

Bonus and Commission Treatment

If you were on track for an annual bonus or had outstanding commissions, your severance agreement may include — or explicitly exclude — these amounts. A full-year target bonus represents significant compensation. Whether it's prorated, excluded entirely, or paid in full is a major variable in the offer's real value.

Equity Acceleration

If you had unvested stock options or RSUs, your severance may include some acceleration of vesting. This is more common in tech companies and more negotiable than most employees realize — especially if you're being laid off close to a vesting cliff.

Outplacement Services

Some employers include career transition or outplacement support — resume help, job search coaching, or access to a placement firm. This has tangible value and is worth requesting if not included.

The Reference Letter Variable

The terms of your employer's reference — what they'll say about you, who is authorized to speak, and what the formal reference policy is — can have lasting value that isn't reflected in the dollar amount of the offer. This is an often-overlooked negotiating point, especially if you left under complicated circumstances.

HOW TO TELL IF YOUR OFFER IS BELOW MARKET

Compare your offer against these signals:

  • Weeks per year: If you're receiving less than 1 week per year of service in a non-executive role, the offer is below typical minimums
  • Total duration: Less than 4 weeks total for any employee with meaningful tenure is a low offer in most industries
  • Benefits cliff: If coverage terminates on your last day with no COBRA subsidy, that's a gap worth negotiating
  • Bonus exclusion: If you're being let go mid-year and the offer explicitly excludes any bonus consideration, this is a common area for negotiation
  • Industry comparison: Tech and finance companies typically pay above the formula; retail and hospitality typically pay at or below it

WHAT IS ACTUALLY NEGOTIABLE

The most consistently negotiable elements of a severance package are: total weeks of pay, benefits continuation duration, bonus proration, equity treatment, the non-compete scope and duration, and the reference letter terms. The least negotiable elements are typically the release of claims language and the confidentiality provisions — employers rarely budge on the core legal protections they're buying.

The fact that an offer is negotiable doesn't mean it will be improved — it means it's worth asking. The most effective negotiation approach is to request a specific, reasonable improvement with a clear rationale, not to reject the offer entirely or make ultimatums.
